Health-Related Fitness Components

The elements of fitness that are essential for preserving your health and keeping you from acquiring various diseases are commonly referred to as the health-related fitness components. These elements of fitness also include your organ systems' ability to function well and their ability to work together to accomplish daily duties and tasks that require effort.

1. Cardiovascular Endurance

Another name for cardiovascular endurance is cardiorespiratory endurance. It is an indicator of overall physical health. It measures how well the heart, lungs, and muscles work together to keep the body active over an extended period. This type of endurance is crucial for performing sustained physical activities without excessive fatigue. A person with high cardiorespiratory endurance can perform high-intensity activities for longer periods without tiring quickly. This endurance is essential for overall health and is a key component of physical fitness.

Organ systems: heart, lungs, & blood vessels.

Butt Kicks

Instead of reaching the knees toward your chest like you do with high knees, you’ll be kicking your feet behind you, reaching your heels toward your butt. This will help with hip flexor mobility and can also loosen up the quads. Simply kick your right foot back toward your right glute, and then kick your left foot back toward your left glute. The faster you go, the more you may feel like you are running in place — so make sure you focus the movement on kicking your feet toward the glute with every step.

3-Minute Step Test

1. Start the metronome counter, and set yourself a timer for 3 minutes.
2. Step on and off the step one foot at a time (up, up, down, down), stepping at a consistent pace to the beat of the metronome counter.
3. Perform this for 3 minutes without stopping.
4. Once the 3 minutes is up, check your pulse.

Jumping Jacks

1. Stand with your feet together and your arms by your side.
2. Jump your feet apart as you raise your arms over your head.
3. Jump back to the starting position and continue this movement.

Side-to-side hops

1. From a standing position, lower your butt down in a squat position.
2. Step your right foot as far over to the right as you can.
3. Then bring your left foot to meet your right foot.
4. Step your left foot as far over to the left as you can.
5. Bring the right foot to meet your left foot.
6. Continue this fluid movement.
7. Keep your butt down low the entire time. Increase your speed or sink into a lower squat to increase the difficulty.

2. Muscular Endurance

Muscular endurance refers to the ability of a muscle or group of muscles to sustain repeated contractions over an extended period. It is an essential component of overall physical fitness, enabling individuals to perform activities that require muscle use over time without fatigue.

Organ System: Musculoskeletal System (bones, muscles, joints, tendons, & ligaments).

Chair Squats

1. Stand just in front of your chair, facing away from it. Your feet should be shoulder-width apart, with toes pointing straight ahead.
2. Keep your spine neutral and head and chest raised. Engage your core as you bend knees and lower your hips down and back. You can lift your arms out in front for extra balance while you lower yourself down.
3. Gently tap the chair with your butt, but don’t sit down.
4. Squeeze glutes and hamstrings to drive your hips forward and up, returning to the starting position.

For a beginner like you, the Chair Squat is the most suitable variation of the standard squat. Its primary objective is to target your lower body's large muscular groups.

Planking

1. Get in a plank position with your upper body off the ground and supported by your elbows and forearms. Your legs should be straight with your weight taken by your toes. Your body should be in a straight line from head to toe.
2. As soon as you are in the correct position, start the timer.
3. Hold this position for as long as you can or until you are unable to hold your back straight or you lower your hips.
4. Record the time.

Lunge

A lunge works the abs, buttocks, hips, and thighs:
1. Stand up straight with the feet together.
2. Bend one knee, lift the opposite leg, step forward onto it, place the foot flat on the floor, and bend the supporting leg, so the knee reaches the bottom.
3. Use the front leg to push back up to the start position and repeat for the opposite leg.

3. Muscular Strength

Muscular strength is the ability of a muscle or muscle group to generate maximal force. It is distinct from muscular endurance, which is the ability of a muscle to sustain repeated contractions over time. Muscular strength is essential for various physical activities, from lifting and carrying objects to performing athletic movements.

Organ System: Musculoskeletal System (bones, muscles, joints, tendons, & ligaments).

One Minute Sit-Ups

1. Lie down on your back, keep your knees bent, and your back and feet flat on the mat.
2. Knees are bent about 90 degrees with both feet flat and no more than 13 inches in front of the buttocks.
3. Raise the head and shoulder until the trunk reaches about a 30-40 degree angle with the exercise surface, then return to the starting position.
4. Perform as many sit-ups as possible in 1 minute.

Toe Stands

Toe stands strengthen your ankles and calves, and you can do them anywhere. Here’s how:
1. Face the back of a chair with your feet shoulder-width apart. Hold onto the chair for balance.
2. Slowly lift yourself up on your toes for a count of four. Pause. Lower slowly. Repeat eight to 10 times for one set.

Push-Ups

Standard push-ups work the chest muscles (pectorals), as well as the shoulder muscles, triceps, and abdominals. To do this exercise:
1. Start in a plank position with your palms directly under your shoulders.
2. Keeping your back flat and bracing your core, lower your body by bending your elbows until your chest almost touches the floor.
3. Immediately push your body back up to the starting position.
4. Count the number of push-ups in one trial only. No rest in between push-ups is allowed.

4. Flexibility

This refers to the full range of motion available in a joint or joints. When you are flexible, your limbs can bend, twist, and move without the risk of getting injured. Flexibility is essential for overall physical health and can improve performance in various activities.

Organ System: Musculoskeletal System (joints, tendons, & ligaments)

Zipper Test

1. Put your right arm up.
2. Bend your right elbow towards your back.
3. Take your left arm to your back by bending your elbow then reach your right hand.
4. Do this on the other side as well: put your left arm up.
5. Bend your left elbow towards your back.
6. Take your right arm to your back by bending your elbow then reach your left hand.

V Sit and Reach

1. Mark a baseline on the floor.
2. Place a ruler perpendicularly to the baseline, crossing it at 15 inches.
3. Sit with your heels on the baseline and feet 12 inches apart.
4. With your legs straight, slowly bend forward to reach the furthest point of the ruler.
5. Hold for 2 seconds and record the distance.
6. Perform three trials, recording the best score.

5. Body Composition

Body composition refers to the percentages of fat, bone, water, and muscle in the human body. It is a more detailed and informative measure of health and fitness than body weight alone because it differentiates between lean mass and fat mass, providing insights into overall health.

Organ System: All organ systems of the body.

Why do you need to improve your body composition?

Improving body composition helps prevent various health-related diseases. Excess body fat, especially in the abdominal area, increases the risk for conditions such as heart disease, high or low insulin levels, high blood pressure, stroke, joint problems, diabetes, blood vessel inflammation, gallbladder disease, certain cancers, and back pain.

How to Improve Body Composition

Maintaining a balanced, sensible diet along with regular exercise is key to reducing excess body fat and achieving a healthier body composition.

BMI Calculation

BMI (Body Mass Index) is a simple index of weight-for-height used to classify underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obesity in adults. It is calculated using the following formula:

BMI = weight (kg) ⁄ height (m)2
