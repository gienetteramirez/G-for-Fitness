Skill-Related Fitness Components

The purpose of skill-related fitness components is to measure an individual's visible ability to perform various sport-related activities, in contrast to health-related fitness components, which focus on the body's overall health and condition.

1. Agility

Agility is the ability to rapidly and efficiently change the direction and position of the body while maintaining control and balance. It involves a combination of speed, coordination, balance, reflexes, and strength. Agility is crucial for many sports and physical activities where quick changes in direction are required.

Ladder Drills (In-In-Out-Out)

1. Stand on the side of the ladder, facing the first box.
2. Step into the first box with your leading leg, followed by your trailing leg.
3. Step backward out of the first box with your leading leg, followed by your trailing leg.
4. Step into the second box with your leading leg, followed by your trailing leg.
5. Continue this pattern through the remainder of the ladder.

Hexagon Agility Test

1. Make a hexagon on the floor.
2. Stand in the center of the hexagon with your feet next to each other.
3. Jump over the line in front of you and then jump back into the hexagon (over the same line).
4. Now jump over the line next to it and jump back again.
5. Repeat until you have jumped over all the lines, keeping your head faced forwards.

Shuttle Run

1. Measure 10 meters and place two marks on both ends.
2. Put two balls or objects at one mark.
3. Stand on the marks with the objects. On the signal “go,” pick up one object, run to the second mark, and place it down.
4. Return, pick up the second object, and transfer it to the second mark. Stop the time when completed.

2. Balance

Balance refers to the ability to maintain equilibrium while moving or stationary. It is divided into static and dynamic balance. Static balance is maintaining your center of mass above your base of support in a stationary position, while dynamic balance is doing so during movement.

Tandem Balance

1. Position one foot on the line.
2. Slowly place the other foot directly in front of the toes of the starting foot.
3. Move along the line, maintaining balance.

Single Leg Balance

1. Stand straight, arms down.
2. Bend one knee and lift your foot up behind you, balancing on the other leg.
3. Hold this position and then switch sides.

Stork Balance

1. Stand on your dominant foot, placing the other foot on the inside of the supporting knee.
2. Raise your heel and maintain balance as long as possible.
3. The stopwatch stops if your hands leave your hips, or you lose balance.

3. Coordination

Coordination is the ability to use different body parts together smoothly and efficiently. It is essential for tasks that require multiple muscle groups working in harmony.

Jump Rope

1. Hold the rope's handles and stand with the rope behind you.
2. Swing the rope over your head, hopping over it as it reaches your feet.
3. Maintain a steady rhythm, using your wrists for control.

1. Hit a crumpled paper alternatively with your palms, aiming above head height.

4. Power

Power refers to the ability to perform one maximum effort in the shortest possible time. It is the product of strength and speed and is a component of fitness that usually involves movements that are explosive in nature. Power can be measured through various tests that assess the ability to perform explosive movements.

Vertical Jump Test

Measures the height of a jump from a standing position to assess lower body power.

1. Stand sideways against a high wall where you can put chalk or tape on. Ensure you stand on a level floor or surface away from any furniture or wall decor for a safe jump. Choose a wall in a room with a high ceiling, at a gym, or outside where you have plenty of space to reach and jump.
• Stand with your dominant side against the wall for a more natural jump and reach.
2. Wrap your middle finger tip on your dominant hand in tape or chalk dust. Use a small piece of masking tape or double-sided tape that can easily stick onto the wall, or use a chalk color that contrasts with the wall color. Your middle finger is your longest finger and will give you the highest reach and jump measurement.
3. Reach as high as you can and mark your reach height on the wall. Keep your feet flat on the ground and reach upward with your taped or chalked hand. At the peak of your reach, touch the wall with your middle finger and leave a mark with the chalk or tape, then measure its height with a tape measure. This is your standing reach.
• Write down your maximum reach measurement before you move into your jumps.
4. Reapply your chalk or tape and take your first standing vertical jump. Stand in the same spot and position as you did to record your reach. Raise your arms over your head and throw yourself down into a squatting position, then jump straight up as quickly as you can. Reach as high as possible and mark the wall again at the peak of your jump.
• Remember to land safely on both feet to avoid injury.
5. Jump 3 to 5 more times with a 1-minute rest between each jump. Do multiple jumps in case you need a few tries to get used to the form or your first jump wasn’t your best. Give yourself time to rest between jumps so your later jump measurements are not affected by fatigue.
• Remember to reapply tape or chalk to your finger and mark the wall each time you jump.
6. Subtract your standing reach from your jump reach. Use a measuring tape to record your highest jump reach, then subtract your standing reach from before. The difference between these measurements is your vertical jump.

Standing Long Jump

Measures the explosive power of the legs.

1. Stand behind a line marked on the ground with feet slightly apart.
2. A two-foot take-off and landing is used, with swinging of the arms and bending of the knees to provide forward drive.
3. The participant attempts to jump as far as possible, landing on both feet.
4. Measure and record the distance from the starting line to the nearest marker made by the participant in the landing surface. There shall be 2 trials.

5. Reaction Time

Reaction time is the interval between the presentation of a stimulus and the initiation of the muscular response to that stimulus. It reflects the speed at which an individual can perceive, process, and respond to information. Reaction time is a crucial component of overall athletic performance and daily functioning.

Object Drop Drill

1. Stand with your hands at your sides while a partner holds an object at shoulder height.
2. Your partner drops the object without warning, and you must catch it before it hits the ground.
3. Increase the difficulty by varying the height or adding multiple objects.

Ruler Drop Test

1. The ruler is held by the assistant between the outstretched index finger and thumb of the athlete's dominant hand so that the top of the athlete's thumb is level with the zero-centimeter line on the ruler.
2. The assistant instructs the participant to catch the ruler as soon as possible after it has been released.
3. The assistant releases the ruler and the participant catches it between their index finger and thumb as quickly as possible.
4. The assistant records the distance between the bottom of the ruler and the top of the participant's thumb where the ruler has been caught.
5. The test is repeated 2 more times, and the average value is used in the assessment.

6. Speed

Speed is the ability to move quickly across the ground or move limbs rapidly to grab or throw. It is a crucial component of athletic performance, particularly in sports that require rapid acceleration, high-velocity movements, or quick reaction times.

Sprint Tests

Timed sprints over set distances, such as 20 meters, 40 meters, or 100 meters, to assess straight-line speed.


30-Meter Dash

Measures acceleration and top-end speed over a short distance.
